Lessons and Legacy

An analysis of this project and the reasons for its creation

I. Introduction

- Overview of the project's purpose: why map the Gulag
- Tension between contemporary relevance and historical distance
- Thesis: Totalitarian systems exist across cultures, languages, and regimes. What does literature reveal that sociological accounts or historical accounts alone cannot?

II. Project Overview & Literature Review

- Project description: what the site does - interactive map - history page - interactive exhibit - images - tools and methods used to build it - Digital humanities context: - precedents for mapping atrocity and forced labor - spatial dimension adds to the study - Introduce the literary corpus: - Solzhenitsyn's The Gulag Archipelago (Soviet gulag, testimonial synthesis) - Viktor Frankl's Man's Search for Meaning (Nazi concentration camps, existential witness) - Anne Applebaum's Gulag: A History (historical-narrative, drawing heavily on memoir and testimony) - Chinese poetry - Etc. - Setting the stage - what commonalities exist between the different cultures experiencing the same terror?

III. What Made the Gulag Possible - A Literary Lens

- Ideological foundations: - Marxist-Leninist class enemy logic - Communist ideology and its implementation in the Soviet Union - compare Frankl's existential approach with Solzhenitsyn's testimonial (moralist) style - Machinery of the Soviet Repression System: - Article 58, NKVD, complicit - Denunciation, arrest, and disappearance - Shared narrative of fear and compliance - Geography as an instrument and metaphor: - Artic Circle, Siberia, and other remote regions - Geographic role in erasing "undesirables" and isolationism - Chinese laogai camps in Xingjian - The "ordinary man": - Evil in every individual - The role of action and inaction - Common thread in Solzhenitsyn and Frankl's work, "dividing" good and evil - Individual freedom and responsibility

IV. Comparative Literature Analysis (Literature as a Witness)

- The problem of testimony: - How to write about extreme suffering, and effectively? - The challenge of conveying the inexpressible - Creating a comprehensive work that combines the author's and others' experiences - Archipelago metaphor: - a chain of islands or camps that are connected but isolated from each other - spatial metaphors are often seen - Responses to suffering: - Frankl's logotheraphy - Solzhenitsyn's approach to depicting suffering and resilience - Other literary responses to suffering and resilience - Common thread of individual responsibility - "With suffering was the birth of responsibility" - What literature does that statistics cannot capture: - Humanizing sheer numbers (e.g. 18 million gulag prisoners) - Ethical stakes in words - Power of testimony and narrative - Work in references to interactive map

V. Conclusion & Future Recommendations

- Restate thesis: "similar consequences and human responses exist towards totalianarianism" - Project limitations: gaps in camp data, translation and access issues, reliablity of data, geographical position of camps - Future directions: adding prisoner testimony layers, deportation routes, interactive government and camp hierarchy, and non-Soviet literary works - Conclusion/reflection: Solzhenitsyn's warning and its relation to modern times
