I. Introduction (200)

- Overview of the project's purpose: why map the Gulag
The Soviet gulag system of the 20th century changed the lives of tens of millions of lives, often for the better but nearly always for the worst. Metaphorically the "archipelago" of camps, being scattered across the U.S.S.R. were similar to a chain of islands; interconnected in nature but separated by distance. Official documentation and records exist noting the statistics, geographic locations, and correspondence at the camps yet all that remains public are on outdated or little-known websites. The inaccessibility of the gulags in the present day, and constant censorship of the darker elements of the Soviet Union's past by Russia, have slowly eroded the moral reminder of this history and its retelling in literature. - Tension between contemporary relevance and historical distance
This tension between contemporary relevance and historical distance brings forth an important question: to what extent should the past be passed over in hopes of creating a better future? Such questions will be explored through literary lens and analyses sections, as will many other ethical questions through authors such as Viktor Frankl and Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn. - Thesis: Totalitarian systems exist across cultures, languages, and regimes. What does literature reveal that sociological accounts or historical accounts alone cannot? This approach pursues what literature reveals that sociological or historical accounts alone cannot in examination of totalitarian systems that exist across cultures, languages, and regimes.

VI. Annotated Bibliography

Primary Sources

  1. Solzhenitsyn, A. I. 2007a. The Gulag Archipelago: An Experiment in Literary Investigation, Volume 1. New York: HarperCollins.
    ———. 2007b. The Gulag Archipelago: An Experiment in Literary Investigation, Volume 2. New York: HarperCollins.
    ———. 2007c. The Gulag Archipelago: An Experiment in Literary Investigation, Volume 3. New York: HarperCollins.

Solzhenitsyn's work is the primary source for understanding the Gulag system and its impact on Soviet society. It combines the personal experiences of hundreds of fellow prisoners with historical and legal analysis of the Soviet state which enabled the camps to function as a tool of repression and control. The work is not only one of historical documentation, but also a personal and moral testament. Winner of the 1970 Nobel Prize in Literature.

  1. Applebaum, Anne. 2003. Gulag: A History. Doubleday Books.

A realist comprehensive history of the Gulag system, with a particular emphasis on life and work within the camps. It focuses on origins, unraveling, and central economic institution the Gulag system became. Winner of the 2004 Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction.
