Mapping the Soviet Gulag "Archipelago"

"Thanks to ideology, the twentieth century was fated to experience evildoing on a scale calculated in the millions. This cannot be denied, nor passed over, not suppressed. How, then, do we dare insist that evildoers do not exist? And who was it that destroyed these millions? Without evildoers there would have been no Archipelago."
— Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, The Gulag Archipelago

Aleksandr Solzhenitsyn, the Russian novelist, historian, and former prisoner of the Soviet gualg system, wrote The Gulag Archipelago to document the experiences of those who lived through the camps. From his own and the testimonies of fellow survivors, Solzhenitsyn sought to lay out the broader consequences not at the hands of a single individual or group, but at the structure of the system itself and the complicity of society.
His work serves as both a historical investigation and a discussion of the moral consequences of political repression. It also serves as a warning to current forms of government that they are not immune to becoming systems of injustice.

History of the Gulag

Soviet Union from the 1920s - 1950s.

Repression

Following a series of reforms after the Bolshevik (October) Revolution of 1917, Soviet government began to classify individuals according to their perceived background and political class. Members of the intelleginsia—including writers, lawmakers, engineers, and many competent specialists—were frequently viewed and targeted due to their social status, level of education, or pre-revoluionary background. As political repression intensified, those not loyal or seen as a threat were removed from their positions, arrested, sent into exile, or headed to the gulag.

Labor

Camp labor came from both convicted criminals and political prisoners. Many camps were located in remote and harsh environments, such as the Artic Circle or Siberia. Inmates endured intense physical work, long hours, and dangerous working conditions—from mining to logging and railroad construction. Survival rates remain highly disputed.

Ideology

Marxist-Leninist ideology became the underlying force shaping Soviet policies. The government frequently took a stance against perceived social and political enemies—such as the kulaks, peasants who had made a decent living—and targeted them due to their success and/or opposition to the state. Broad legal provisions, such as the frequently cited Article 58-1, were used to "protect the state" from dangerous individuals though their expansive language and loose interpretation: "A counter-revolutionary action is any action aimed at overthrowing, undermining or weakening of the power of workers' and peasants' Soviets..."
